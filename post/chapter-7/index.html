Customer Churn Modelling

June 14, 2019

Introduction Customer Churn refers to the rate of customer attrition in a company or in simpler words speed at which customer leaves your company or service. Examples of customer churn includes Cancelation of a subscription • Closure of an account • Non-renewal of a contract or service agreement • Decision to shop at another store • Use another service provider Churn could happen due to many different reasons and churn analysis helps to identify the cause (and timing) of this churn opening up opportunities to implement effective retention strategies. So what we will do is:

  1. Gather available customer behavior, transactions, demographics data and usage patterns
  2. Utilize these data points to predict customer segments who are likely to churn
  3. Create a model to pattern the risk tolerance of the business with respect to churn probability.
  4. Design an intervention model to consider how the level of intervention could affect the churn percentages and customer lifetime value (CLV)
  5. Implement effective experimentation across multiple customer segments for reducing churn and promoting retention. Without a strong understanding of your customers and their behaviors, it’s hard to retain them so the first step in creating this model is understanding your customer behavior from customer data points. Lets see what kind of data do we need to to asses the triggers that caused them to ultimately leave your company.

Customer Information • Zip Code Income Bracket Gender Occupation Do they have kids in the household? How do they find your website/ product? Do they open your newsletters, and other trigger emails or click on any links? Products • Type of Product Variety of Products Coupon Usage Product Preferences or Combos Purchase History • Frequency of Purchase Date of Last Purchase Time of Day/Season of Purchase Value of Purchases Payment Methods Balances / Store Credit Customer Interactions • Service Questions Store Visits / Online Complaint Resolutions Complaint Priority How do they complain — email or phone or twitter? Frequency of complaints These are some just sample fields to get started and we prefer more. It’s important to know as much as we can about our customers to know what event lead them to leaving and finding the next competitor. The more relevant data you gather, the more accurate your model will be. If you use machine learning enabled churn models, you can use more variables than is humanly possible to compute and play with. As soon as you have these varied but connected customer data in one place so as to easily manipulate and query, you will see trends emerge in front of your eyes that will give you insights into customer churn. The consolidated data of all the churned customers will also help you to group behaviours and establish patterns.

DATA PREPARATION Complete- Do you have all the relevant dimensions? What percent of data have missing values or nulls? You can fill-in some of the missing values by data exploration.e.g., values of ‘State’ based on customer address; ‘Product Category’ based on the product item etc. Clean- Do you have multiple values for the same dimension? E.g., CA/Calif/California; Healthcare/Hospital/Provider; different abbreviations for a product name etc. If yes, you can do some data cleansing to ensure uniformity. Accurate- Are there negative revenue values or $0 revenues for some transactions?; Date conflicts; ‘NONE’ or ‘N/A’ values etc. You can decide, after discussing with the stakeholders, whether to include or exclude such bad data in the analysis. In predictive+diagnostic analytics, there is one more step involved in data preparation- creating the target variable. Incase of churn analysis, it could be a binary column such as “will churn?” .You can fill in the values for this variable by analyzing the historical data. E.g., a value of 1/TRUE for customers who cancelled their subscription and 0/FALSE for those who renewed. • We have used keras and tensorlow library to implement this project and The Prediction engine is built over a deep Artificial Neural Network backed with Keras. • I have achieved an accuracy of around ~85% on both training and testing data. I used a very simple neural network. Please note that the data is in tabular format, hence we don’t need to use complicated architectures which would lead to overfitting. I used two dense layers with 64 neurons and 8 neurons with relu as the activation function. input_dim argument denotes the number of features in the dataset or in other words the number of columns present in the dataset. In between, I used 20% dropouts to reduce overfitting. The dropout layer ensures that we remove a set percentage of the data each time we iterate through the neural network. kernel_constraint is used for scaling of the weights present in the neural network. The last layer is also a dense layer with 1 neuron and sigmoid as the activation function.

• I trained the model for 50 epochs with a batch size value of 8. One epoch is when an entire dataset is passed forward and backward through the neural network only once. Batch size is the total number of training examples present in a single batch. • The ANN is trained over K-fold cross validation testing over 10 rounds to find if it was underfit or overfit over the data based on the variance between the accuracies of the 10 rotations. • The model is Tuned over the Hyperparameters to find the best batch size, epoch and optimizer for generating the best possible combination for best fit model.

• The final prediction of the model is the percentage of churn for that customer. • The prediction signifies the chances of the customer to leave the services of the bank which makes the bank to focus more on such such customers and try to retain them using Sales and Marketing strategies
